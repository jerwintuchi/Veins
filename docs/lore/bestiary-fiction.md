# Bestiary (Fiction)

> **Status:** Drafted (canon framework).
> **See also:** [cosmology.md](cosmology.md) · [choirs.md](choirs.md) · [../systems/incarnates.md](../systems/incarnates.md)

## Purpose

What the players actually face, told as fiction rather than mechanics: how a rumor
becomes a Manifestation, a Manifestation becomes an Incarnate, and why the only
honest way to know one is to read it.

## Design Philosophy

### Manifestation to Incarnate

A **Manifestation** is an unconfirmed phenomenon: a report, a rumor, a thing seen in
the decay that has not yet been pinned down. It is born of **belief, sin, or a broken
relic** (the three families from [cosmology.md](cosmology.md)). The Collegium does not
yet know what it is, only that it is there and that it is dangerous.

When scholars have observed a Manifestation closely enough to defend a classification
of it, the Archive confirms it and it is named an **Incarnate**: something made flesh.
The theological weight of the word is deliberate. In a world with a real, reigning
God, naming a thing "incarnate" is a heavy claim, and the Choirs argue about whether
the Collegium has any right to make it.

### Why it cannot be memorized

Here is the core of the fiction and the game at once: **there is no settled answer to
what any Incarnate truly is.** The Archive records *observable facts* (a sign on the
stone, a reaction to flame, a behavior under stress) and the *interpretations* scholars
have defended from them. It does not record truth, because the truth is not available
to anyone below God.

So a scholar in the field cannot recall "this is the monster that does X." Every
Incarnate is a fresh body of evidence to be read. Prior records sharpen the *language*
of reading (what signs tend to mean) but never substitute for reading *this one*. This
is exactly the sign-language mechanic: the world enforces interpretation because the
world genuinely does not know.

### The three classification schools

The Collegium's serious factions each explain Incarnates differently. None is proven;
each maps to a [Choir](choirs.md).

- **Judgment (Penitential view):** Incarnates are sin made flesh, the material edge of
  divine judgment. To them, redemption is possible because the thing is, at root, a moral fact.
- **Sanctity (Reliquary view):** Incarnates are sanctity spilled from broken holy
  objects, sacred power without its vessel. To them, containment and the relic matter most.
- **Meaning (Doxic view):** Incarnates are congealed belief, a plague of meaning with no
  author. To them, only measurement and dispassionate record are honest.

A single Incarnate can be argued into any of the three by a clever scholar, which is
why expeditions end in debate as often as in certainty.

### Mutation, in fiction

The decay is not stable, so neither are Incarnates. A **mutation** is the same
phenomenon re-emerged altered: signs shifted, a new aspect surfaced, an old reading
made wrong. Fictionally it is why the Archive can never close a case for good.

## Non-negotiable Rules

1. No Incarnate's "true nature" is ever revealed by the text. Keep all three schools live.
2. Records are observable facts plus defended interpretation, never certified truth.
3. The horror is judgment and uncertainty under a real God, not meaninglessness.

## Implementation Notes

- The three families (belief, sin, broken relic) and three schools (Meaning, Judgment,
  Sanctity) are the fictional source for the data-driven
  [incarnate catalog](../content/incarnate-catalog.md) and its sign vocabularies.
- "Read this one, every time" is the [sign language](../systems/sign-language.md) plus
  [probing](../systems/investigation-and-probing.md); mutation is the
  [mutations](../content/mutations.md) content axis.
